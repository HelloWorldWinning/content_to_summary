Methods and Results: In vitro, Western blotting of nuclear fractions of bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMDMs) found DRF treatment increased antioxidant transcription factor Nrf2 (2-fold, P < 0.001) and decreased inflammatory transcription factor NF-kappa B(-58%, P < 0.05). In vivo, using the tandem stenosis model of unstable plaque, daily gavage of DRF (100 mg/kg) reduced plaque area (-51%, P < 0.001), Oil Red O+ lipid content (-52%, P < 0.001) and necrotic core area (-48%, P < 0.01), when compared to vehicle controls. Plaques of DRF-treated mice had fewer CD68+ macrophages (-68%, P < 0.0005) and a reduction in Ter119+ erythrocytes, an intraplaque haemorrhage marker (-75%, P < 0.05). Flow cytometry analyses of blood revealed there were fewer circulating proatherogenic cells including Gr1+Ly6G+ neutrophils (-60%, P < 0.001), Ly6C+CCR2+ activated monocytes (-28%, P < 0.05) and CD8+ natural killer T-cells (-37%, P < 0.01) in DRF treated mice. Measurement of Nrf2-activated antioxidant genes by qPCR found DRF treatment increased aortic arch Gsta3 (+46%, P <0.01) and liver Nqo1 (+31%, P < 0.05), Sod1 (+32%, P < 0.01) and Ho1 (+21%, P < 0.05) mRNA levels. Conclusion: DRF has atheroprotective properties with implications for repurposing DRF as a new therapy to prevent atherosclerosis
