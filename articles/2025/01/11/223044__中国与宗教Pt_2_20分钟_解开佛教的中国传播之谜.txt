**Summary:**

1.  **Introduction:**
    *   The content discusses why Buddhism, despite its significant influence in Chinese history, did not become the dominant religion like in Thailand or Japan.
    *   It challenges the sole explanation of Confucianism's emphasis on filial piety and introduces other factors.

2.  **Origins of Chinese Buddhism:**
    *   Chinese Buddhism is a "second-hand" import, initially from India to Central Asia, then to China.
    *   It originated from India's reaction against early Buddhism due to new dynasty’s support for Brahmanism. Buddhist refugees fled to Central Asia.
    *   Alexander the Great's influence in Gandhara led to the creation of Buddha statues, integrating Greek artistic styles, which was critical for acceptance in China.

3.  **Chinese Cultural Context:**
    *   Unlike the abstract nature of early Buddhism in India, China valued practical applications, idol worship, and integration with existing beliefs.
    *   China’s social and political system was centered around family and ancestor veneration, focused on this life rather than the afterlife, creating a conflict with core Buddhist concepts.

4.  **Factors for Buddhism's Acceptance:**
    *   **Idolization:** The introduction of Buddha statues satisfied the Chinese need for concrete images of deities.
    *   **Practical Use:** Early Buddhist practices included fortune-telling and rituals that offered practical solutions.
    *   **Localization:** Adapting to Chinese cultural norms, such as using the term "Tian Zhu" (Lord of Heaven) for God, was essential.

5.  **Impact of Chaos and Politics:**
    *   Buddhism gained prominence during the chaos of the late Eastern Han dynasty and the subsequent Three Kingdoms and Northern and Southern Dynasties period.
    *   In times of turmoil, Buddhism’s idea of Karma and reincarnation offered solace and an explanation for suffering not tied to ancestral actions.
    *   The economic strength of monasteries (寺院经济) became a critical factor in attracting political support and the development of Chinese Buddhism.

6.  **Clash with Confucianism & Daoism:**
    *   Confucianism, deeply rooted in family and lineage, clashed with Buddhist ideas of detachment and reincarnation.
    *   Daoism, although not as politically influential, posed a threat to Buddhist's position.
    *   Daoism, with its rebelliousness and political ambitions, was more threatening to the ruling power than Buddhism.

7.  **Unique Cases & Power Struggles:**
    *   The Tang Dynasty’s unique situation, with its rulers of mixed heritage, led to the adoption of Daoism, associating the ruling family with Lao Tzu.
    *   The Liang Dynasty's Emperor Wu, initially promoting Buddhism for political leverage, became overly engrossed, leading to unintended consequences.
    *   Empress Wu Zetian promoted Buddhism to diminish Daoist power and legitimize her rule by patronizing Buddhist art and temples.

8.  **Power Dynamics of Religion:**
    *   The success or failure of a religion in China depended on its utility to the ruling power.
    *   The Emperor could use or destroy religion according to his goals, unlike the European relationship between church and state.
    *   China’s version of “Mandate of Heaven” is about results, not divinity. Whoever controls power is legitimate.

9.  **Conclusion**
    *  **Core Point:** Buddhism's failure to become the dominant religion in China is due to a complex interplay of cultural values, political opportunism, and its inherent conflict with the Chinese worldview centered on ancestor veneration and family structure.
    *   **Fundamental Point:** In China, the relationship between religion and power is characterized by the ruler using religion as a tool to consolidate power, rather than the other way around.
    *  **Overarching Framework:** The historical narrative of Buddhism in China is shaped by the intersection of cultural values, political opportunism and economic power dynamics, where its success or failure is determined by its utility to those in power.
