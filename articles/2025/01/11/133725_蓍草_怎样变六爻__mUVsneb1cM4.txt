*  **A. Ancient Practices:**
    *   Introduces two key ancient practices: sacrifice (祭祀) and warfare (战争).
    * Focuses on the complex practice of sacrifice and its predictive aspects.
*   **B. Yarrow Stalk Divination:**
    *   Explores the use of yarrow stalks (蓍草) for divination.
    *   Traces the use of yarrow stalks back to ancient legends, linking them to sacred places and figures such as Fuxi.
   *   Yarrow stalks, associated with longevity, are seen as capable of perceiving the future.
   * This mirrors seeking advice from elders and demonstrates how the lifespan of the plant is directly related to the concept of longevity and time itself.
* **C. The Mechanics of Divination:**
    *   Introduces the mathematical principles behind the yarrow stalk method using the 50 - 49 principle.
    *   The 50 stalks is reduced to 49 to create binary possibilities (yin and yang) to simulate change.
    *  Explains the structured process of dividing the stalks to produce a hexagram.

**IV. The Yi Jing's Method: A Detailed Walkthrough**

*  **A. The Divination Process:**
    *   Detailed step-by-step explanation of how yarrow stalks are manipulated to create hexagrams.
    *   Describes how the numbers generated through each step are combined to form individual lines (yao) of the hexagram.
    *   Explains how the resulting hexagram is interpreted to gain insight about a situation.
   * Each phase of the process is described with a clear and concise explanation and the resulting implications in terms of yin and yang.
*  **B. Example of Hexagram:**
    * The process is detailed using the Kūn (困) Hexagram as an example.
    * Details on how the structure of the hexagram and its meaning is used to provide a solution.
* **C. Subjectivity vs. Objectivity:**
    *   Highlights that interpretation of the results is subjective.
    *  The answers can be tailored to fit interpretations.
   *   Emphasizes that the *Yi Jing* is more than a divinatory tool; it offers insight into the cyclical nature of life.

**V. The Essence of "Yi" (Change)**

*   **A. Definition of Change:**
    *   Explores the concept of "Yi" (易) as a representation of constant change and transformation.
    *  Identifies "Yi" as "阴阳交替的生生变化之道" (the way of ceaseless transformation through the interplay of yin and yang).
*   **B. Three Key Meanings:**
    *   "Yi" embodies transformation, simplicity, and stability of fundamental laws.
    *   Simplicity (简易) implies focusing on the essence of matters, as in "抓住根本要害" (grasping the core).
    *   Stability (不易) represents the unchanging, essential principles of the world.
*  **C. The Chinese Way of Thinking:**
    *  Highlights how Chinese thinking is characterized by combining change, simplicity, and stability.
    *  The author discusses how the combination is difficult for Westerners to understand.

**VI. Confucius and the *Yi Jing***

*   **A. Confucius's Later Interest:**
    *   Discusses Confucius's late-life passion for the *Yi Jing*.
    *   Contrasts this with his earlier skepticism.
*   **B. Understanding Deeper Meaning:**
    *   Confucius sought the essence of the text beyond its divination aspects, focusing on the text itself.
   *   He found deeper meaning in the *Yi Jing*'s structure and content as it reflected the world.
* **C. Confucian Understanding:**
    *   Confucius understood that the hexagram's words, created by King Wen, were meant to be secret.
    *   He felt King Wen, who was in political hardship, was using the hexagrams to pass down his teachings.
*   **D. Confucius's Evolution:**
    *   Confucius’s views underwent a transformation from initial rejection to eventual deep study.
    *   He saw it as a way to cultivate virtues and improve oneself.
    *  By focusing on personal growth, he was able to see the potential of the text.

**VII. The Deeper Purpose of Yi: Self-Improvement**

*  **A.  The Power of Change:**
   *   Confucius believed that the *Yi Jing* could improve people.
  *  Even the simple-minded could benefit from its wisdom.
  * Those who engage in deception would come to know the limits of their scheming.
*   **B. Divination vs. Deeper Understanding:**
    *   Confucius viewed divination as a secondary aspect, valuing the deeper understanding of change and self-cultivation.
  *  The essence was not in the divination but in the way it changed a person's spirit.
    *  He saw divination as a tool to understand the fundamental nature of numbers.
* **C. The Ultimate Goal:**
    *  The *Yi Jing* encourages inner reflection, self-love, and acting with love for others.
    *  This means that understanding comes before taking action and that action should be in line with the principles of the text.
   *   The author concludes that these ideas are similar to Kant's ideas of reason, autonomy, and duty.
   *  He notes that the ultimate goal is to "异人轮异里" (change a person's inner character).

**VIII. Conclusion:**

*   The *Yi Jing* has been adapted into many different schools of thought such as Confucianism and Taoism.
*  This adaptation of interpretation leads to the need for a more critical approach to the text.
*   The goal of the text is the same despite the many interpretations of it, to better ourselves.

**Core Point:** The *Yi Jing* is fundamentally a profound text about the nature of change, human understanding, and self-cultivation, rather than just a book of divination.

**Fundamental Point:** The *Yi Jing* is not primarily a tool for fortune-telling, but a means for understanding life's constant transformations, discovering inner harmony, and aligning one's actions with moral principles.
