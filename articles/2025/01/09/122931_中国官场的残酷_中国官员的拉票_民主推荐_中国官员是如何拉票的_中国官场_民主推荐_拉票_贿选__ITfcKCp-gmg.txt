**I. The Complexity of Chinese Official Appointments:**

*   **Theoretical vs. Practical Power:** While a top official (一把手) theoretically has the power to appoint subordinates, in reality, they are often influenced by superiors and peers.
*   **Influence from Above:** Even a powerful line secretary (线纽书记) is subject to pressures from higher-ranking officials like the party secretary (神委书记) and mayor (市长), who may recommend their own candidates.
*   **Internal Competition:**  The appointment of officials is often a difficult balancing act, even for the highest-ranking officials. For example, the top official may have to choose among candidates recommended by the second and third highest officials.
*   **Case Study:**  The example of a retiring high school principal's successor illustrates the complicated nature of official appointments. The line secretary must contend with competing recommendations from party and governmental leaders and even higher officials from Beijing.
*   **"Democratic Recommendation":**  A system of "democratic recommendation" (民主推荐) exists, which is a type of limited election process, but is ultimately subject to approval of senior officials (书记办公会 and 长委会). This is a formal election of sorts, but also allows political influence, and candidates campaign (拉票).

**II. The "Democratic Recommendation" (Limited Election) Process:**

*   **Candidate Selection:**  The process begins with a "democratic recommendation" that aims to be broad and professional.
*   **Voting Groups:**  The group of people who can vote in this process includes a wide range of people including the leader of the department, sub-leaders, staff, and people that have business relations with that department.
*   **Formal Approval:**  The election results are not necessarily final. They need to be approved by party and government meetings. These meetings can potentially overturn the "election" results.
*   **Balancing Act:** The process is designed to balance different influences and maintain stability within the system.
*   **Importance for Officials:** This kind of “election” is critical for the career of officials. Candidates often will try to gain votes through all kinds of means.

**III.  The Reality of Competition and "Ticket Buying" (拉票):**

*   **Limited Opportunities:**  Promotion opportunities for officials are limited and competitive.
*   **Stiff Competition:** Officials will go to great lengths and use many different approaches to compete for a position.
*   **Age Limits:**  Age restrictions for promotions increase the intensity of competition.
*   **System Complexity:** There are also multiple factors influencing one's promotion, which are not limited to one’s capability but also include their relationships with others.
*   **Ticket Buying as Standard Practice:**  The "democratic recommendation" process has led to a widespread practice of "ticket buying" (拉票), where officials solicit votes by offering favors, gifts, and money.

**IV.  The Consequences of Ticket Buying (拉票):**

*   **Financial Burden:** Ticket buying is very expensive, as officials have to spend money on gifts and dinners for potential voters.
*   **Health Issues:**  The stress and excessive social engagements associated with ticket buying can lead to health problems.
*   **Corrupted Promotion:** This process may result in the election of less capable officials who are better at playing the political game than performing their duties.
*   **Damaged Public Image:** Ticket buying harms the image of officials.
*   **Political Weapon:** Ticket buying can be used as a tool in political infighting, as evidenced by the case of the line office director and vice county chief.

**V. Critical Reflection on System**

*   The system is not based on competence or merit but on who can build connections and buy votes.
*   The author questions whether "one person, one vote" elections can truly benefit the general public and lead to the election of competent and virtuous officials.

**Fundamental Point:** The system of "democratic recommendation" in China does not guarantee a fair selection based on merit and is vulnerable to manipulation by political and corrupt means, making the election process less effective than what it should be.
