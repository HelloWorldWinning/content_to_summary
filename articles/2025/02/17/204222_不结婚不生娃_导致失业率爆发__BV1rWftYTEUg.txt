**Outline and Summary:**

1.  **Premise:** People are realizing the limited practical value of higher education in the current economic climate.
2.  **Cost vs. Benefit Analysis:**
    *   Significant financial investment (hundreds of thousands to millions) in education from kindergarten to university.
    *   The return on investment is often low, resulting in unemployment or low-paying jobs (e.g., 3000/month).
3.  **Who Benefits from the Education System?**
    *   Teachers and professors (especially at the university level).
    *   Educational institutions (tutoring centers, specialized training).
    *   The real estate market (high-priced school district housing).
4.  **Alternative Paths:**
    *   Entering the workforce or vocational training (e.g., skilled trades) directly after high school may be more economically viable.
    *   These alternatives can lead to higher-paying jobs than many university degrees.
5.  **The Cycle of Contribution:**
    *   The education system delays entry into the workforce, creating a cycle of spending and contributing to the economy without personal gain.
    *   This cycle is compared to the cycle of working for capital after marriage and childbirth, where individuals contribute without receiving proportionate benefits.
6.  **Consequences of this realization:**
    *   The market demands will decrease and that would affect the stability of the economic system.
    *   High unemployement rate will leads to increase of societal problems, such as poverty, crime, etc.

**Core Point:** The substantial investment in higher education often yields inadequate financial returns, prompting reflection on alternative pathways and the beneficiaries of the current system.

**Overarching Framework:** This content analyzes the economic efficiency of higher education as a personal investment, questioning its value proposition in the context of current employment realities and comparing it to other forms of economic contribution.
