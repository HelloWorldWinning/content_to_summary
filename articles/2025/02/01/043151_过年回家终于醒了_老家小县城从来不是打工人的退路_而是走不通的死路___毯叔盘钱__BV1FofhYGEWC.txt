## Summary

**I. Core Ideas:**

1.  **The "Escape to Hometown" Myth:** Many young people in large cities, feeling stressed and disillusioned, consider returning to their small hometowns as a refuge. However, this idea is often based on a romanticized view and not on a realistic assessment.
2.  **Limited Opportunities in Small Cities:**
    *   **Resource Monopoly:** Local elites and families with connections control most resources, job opportunities, and profitable ventures.
    *   **Limited Job Market:** The job market for those without connections is often limited to low-paying and low-skill jobs.
    *   **Low Salaries and High Cost of Living:** Wages in small cities are very low, even as the cost of living remains comparable to larger cities.
3.  **The Illusion of "Lying Flat":** The idea of simply finding a low-paying job and relaxing in a small town is unrealistic due to the combination of low income, high living expenses, and the financial burden of supporting family.
4.  **Small Cities' Bleak Future:**
    *   **Economic Decline:** Many small cities lack sustainable industries and are experiencing economic and population declines, leading to limited job prospects.
    *   **Population Drain:** Young people are leaving for larger cities, leading to a decline in the workforce and birth rates and an aging population.
    *   **Lack of Resources:** Limited financial resources and talent drain creates an environment where growth is unlikely and decline is probable.
5.  **Staying in the City as the Better Option:** Despite the challenges, staying in a larger city offers more opportunities for personal and professional growth. It provides a level playing field compared to the stagnant and nepotistic nature of small cities.

**II. Core Point:**

Small hometowns are not a viable retreat for ordinary people from big cities, but rather a potential trap due to their limited resources, opportunities, and economic stagnation.

**III. Fundamental Point:**

For individuals from ordinary families, staying and working hard in large cities is a more pragmatic path for upward mobility compared to returning to small hometowns.

**IV. Overarching Framework:**

The content presents a critical analysis of the common desire among urbanites to return to small towns, examining the realities of economic and social structures in these areas to discourage the idea of a “retreat” and encourage continued pursuit of opportunities in larger cities, based on an analysis of economic structures, job market, and social structures.
