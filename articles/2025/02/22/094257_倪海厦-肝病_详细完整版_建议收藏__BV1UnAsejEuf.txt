    *   Addressing bowel movements is a top priority in all TCM methods.

3.  **Yin-Yang Imbalance:**
    *   The concept of Yang Qi returning to the liver at night, potentially causing heat sensations if there are blockages.

4.  **The Role of Bowel Movements (Constipation):**
    *   The importance of bowel movements for overall health and in the context of liver treatment.
    *   The impact of emotional factors and shallow breathing on bowel regularity.
    *   The need for "Su Gan" (soothing the liver) and addressing dryness to promote bowel movements.

    *   The explanation of various herbs to promote bowel movement, such as "Bai Shao"
    *   The use of formulas like "Gui Zhi Tang" and "Yin Chen Hao Tang" to balance Yin and Yang and address specific symptoms.
    *   Adjustments of dosages and formulas based on the patient's response and evolving symptoms.
    *   Emphasis on the importance of spleen and the herbal ingredients that helps with spleen and liver.

6.  **Case Study Continuation:**
    *   Details of the patient's ongoing treatment, including adjustments to herbal formulas, and monitoring of symptoms.
    *   Addressing issues like constipation, night sweats, and discomfort.

7.  **Long-term Follow-up and Considerations:**
    *   The patient's experience with surgery and the role of TCM in post-operative care.
    *   The importance of "Yang Pan" (Yang plate) and gauging the body's reactive ability of Yin and Yang.
    *   The significance of not rushing into drastic procedures and weighing the potential risks and benefits.

8.  **Addressing the deeper health condition after relieving surface/acute symptoms**
    *   Addressing the deeper health condition after relieving surface/acute symptoms is as critical as it is to make it go away. For instance, after the constipation, the doctor should find out if the root of the liver condition is solved as well.
